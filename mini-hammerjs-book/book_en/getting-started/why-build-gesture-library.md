# Why Build a Gesture Library Yourself?

In the previous chapter, we learned the value of gesture libraries—they free us from the tedious work of handling low-level events. At this point, a rational voice might whisper in your head: "Since mature wheels like `Hammer.js` already exist, why should we reinvent one?"

This is an excellent question. In software engineering, "Don't Reinvent the Wheel" is a well-known principle. It advises us to make good use of existing, battle-tested tools from the community to improve development efficiency.

However, the second half of this principle is often overlooked: **"Unless you want to learn more than just how to use the wheel, but how to build the car."**

This is the core purpose of our journey. We are not here to "reinvent" a wheel, but to achieve deeper growth by "building" it.

## 1. The Best Way to Learn is to "Learn by Creating"

There are generally three levels of learning a technology:

*   **Level One: Knowing "how to use" it.** This is the stage where most developers are. We read the documentation, learn the APIs, and use them to complete business requirements. This is like learning how to drive a car.
*   **Level Two: Understanding its "principles".** We understand the internal workings of the tool by reading its source code or technical articles. This is like not only knowing how to drive but also having read the car's repair manual, understanding how the engine and gearbox work.
*   **Level Three: Being able to "create" it yourself.** We can implement the core features of the tool from scratch. This is like assembling, or even designing, a car ourselves. Only by reaching this level can we truly say we have "mastered" it.

By directly using `Hammer.js`, we stay at the first level; by reading its source code, we can reach the second. But only when we build a `mini-hammer.js` ourselves can we truly reach the third level. In this process, concepts that were once vague and abstract—like state machines, event flows, and the adapter pattern—will become incredibly clear and concrete.

## 2. Gaining the Confidence of a Master Butcher

Many developers feel overwhelmed and intimidated when faced with a large and complex codebase (like React or Webpack). This fear stems from the unknown.

The process of "building a wheel" is the best way to overcome this fear. Once you have implemented the core architecture of a gesture library yourself, when you look at the source code of `Hammer.js` or any other gesture library, you will no longer be "looking up" but "looking at it on the same level."

You will find that although its code is more complete and its edge-case handling is more elegant, its core design philosophy and module division are remarkably similar to the `mini-hammer.js` you built. You will be able to quickly locate the core code, understand its design intent, and even find areas for improvement.

This ability to dissect and understand the essence of things, like a master butcher, is a key indicator of a developer's transition from "junior/intermediate" to "senior." The confidence it brings will far surpass the sense of accomplishment from completing an ordinary business project.

## 3. The Joy Comes from Creation Itself

Last but not least, creation itself is a joyful thing.

In our daily business development, we are more often "implementing" requirements. "Building a wheel," on the other hand, gives us the opportunity to think and create like a true "architect." We can freely design APIs, plan modules, and implement algorithms. In this process, we are the masters of our own world.

Starting from a blank file, as you add lines of code, a living "entity" that responds to your fingertips gradually comes to life. The pure joy and sense of achievement you feel when you successfully recognize a "pinch-to-zoom" gesture for the first time on the screen, using the library you wrote, is beyond what any words can describe.

So, let's temporarily set aside the obsession with "efficiency" and embark on this building journey with the mindset of an "apprentice." This journey may not directly produce the "wheel" you need for your next project, but it will build you a valuable "toolbox" that will enable you to build any "vehicle" in the future.
